Part 1: Data Prep & EDA

This section covers how raw credit data was cleaned and shaped into a population worth testing and what that population revealed before a single message was considered.

View full notebook on GitHub

The Dataset

This project needed a population of people who had recently missed a payment. The UCI Default of Credit Card Clients dataset of 30,000 credit card holders was used as a stand-in for a healthcare billing population, filtered down to the 6,355 members who were one or two months behind on payments. Recently delinquent enough that outreach could still move the needle.

Using a proxy dataset is a documented limitation. The methodology and rollout framework apply directly to a live billing environment with real member data.

Population after filtering:

Metric Value
Total experiment population 6,355 members
1-month delinquent 3,688 (58%)
2-month delinquent 2,667 (42%)
Baseline recovery rate 51.3%

Data Prep

Education and marital status fields contained undocumented codes that were mapped to known categories. All 24 column names were renamed for readability. The outcome variable was reframed from "defaulted next month" to "recovered," meaning a member who resolved their balance within the observation window.

Six behavioral and demographic features were then created to describe each member in a way that would be meaningful for the experiment.

Engineered features:

Feature Description Why it matters
Balance tier Low, Mid, High based on outstanding balance Higher balances may respond differently to tone
First-time miss Member has no prior delinquency history First-timers are most likely to respond to empathetic outreach
Prior delinquency count Number of delinquent months in the prior 5 months Chronic delinquents may be less message-sensitive
High risk flag High balance or 2+ prior delinquent months Useful for conservative rollout decisions
Age band 18-29, 30-39, 40-49, 50+ Controls for demographic variation in the experiment
Delinquency month 1-month or 2-month delinquent Captures urgency and recency of the missed payment

The most important of these is first-time miss. A member who has never missed a payment before is a fundamentally different audience than someone with a pattern of delinquency.

The cleaning and feature engineering logic lives in two standalone Python modules, data_prep.py and feature_engineering.py, rather than inside the notebook. This keeps the analysis clean and makes the pipeline reusable on any new dataset without touching the experiment or modeling code.

What the Data Revealed

Finding 1: Recovery rate splits sharply by segment
Out of every 100 first-time missers, 64 resolved their outstanding balance and paid. Out of every 100 repeat missers, only 44 did. That is a 20-percentage-point gap within the same population, before any message is sent. It means these two groups are not the same audience and should not be treated as one.

Finding 2: The more times someone has missed, the less likely they are to pay
Recovery rate drops consistently as prior delinquency increases, from 63.9% for members with no payment history issues down to 31.6% for members who missed payments in every one of the prior five months. Members at that low end likely need a different intervention entirely. A better-worded message is probably not enough.

Finding 3: Balance size determines where the dollar impact lives
Low-balance members recover at the highest rate but carry the smallest individual balances. High-balance members represent $4.61M in total outstanding balance but recover at a lower rate. The middle tier is where a messaging intervention generates the most dollar impact per additional recovery.

Balance tier breakdown:

Balance Tier Recovery Rate Total Balance n
Low (under $900 USD) 57.5% $925K 3,905
Mid ($900 to $3,100 USD) 40.5% $2.94M 1,644
High (over $3,100 USD) 43.1% $4.61M 806

First-time missers with low balances recover at 70.1%, the highest rate in the entire population. Repeat missers show far less variation across balance tiers, ranging from 41.3% to 45.3%. Their behavior is more consistent, and consistently harder to move with messaging alone.

Experiment Design

Before the experiment ran, one key question had to be answered: how big does the lift need to be to actually matter? A 1 or 2 percentage point improvement might be real but not worth the effort of changing the outreach program. Three percentage points was the judgment call, the smallest improvement that would justify retraining staff, updating templates, and managing a rollout.

From there, a calculation called a power analysis determined how many members were needed to detect that improvement reliably. Think of it like a sample size calculator. Feed it the baseline recovery rate, the minimum improvement you care about, and how confident you want to be in the result, and it tells you the minimum number of members needed. That number came out to 4,992 total. With 6,355 available, the experiment had more than enough.

Were the two groups actually balanced?

Once members were assigned, a statistical check confirmed the two groups were genuinely equivalent before the experiment ran. Think of it like checking that two sports teams are evenly matched before the game starts. If one group happened to have significantly more first-time missers or high-balance members, any difference in results could be explained by group composition rather than the message.

The check produces a p-value for each variable, a number between 0 and 1 that measures how similar the two groups are on that characteristic. A p-value of 1.0 means the groups are perfectly matched. A p-value below 0.05 would signal a meaningful imbalance worth investigating. Every variable came back well above that threshold. Both groups entered the experiment on equal footing.

Balance validation results:

Variable p-value Result
First-time miss 1.0000 Pass
Balance tier 0.9956 Pass
High risk 1.0000 Pass
Delinquency month 1.0000 Pass
Sex 0.9591 Pass

All five variables passed. The two groups entered the experiment on equal footing, which means any difference in recovery rate that emerges can be attributed to the message and not to a flaw in how members were assigned.

The 51.3% baseline recovery rate becomes the starting point for the Bayesian model in Part 2. The segment findings, particularly the first-time miss gap, drive both the stratification strategy and the phased rollout recommendation in Part 3. And the $4.9M in unrecovered balances gives the business impact model its dollar foundation.
