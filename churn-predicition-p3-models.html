Part 3: Churn Prediction Modeling

Turning exploration into prediction

This section focuses on building forecasting tools to spot members at risk of churning before they actually leave. Member behavior and characteristics are used to train machine learning models, estimating individual churn risk and highlighting key influencing factors. The goal is to move from seeing patterns to acting proactively. View full notebook on GitHub

Modeling Approach: Choosing the Right Tools

To predict member churn, two main types of machine learning models were explored: Logistic Regression and Random Forest. These models learn from data prepared in Part 2: Churn EDA, including engagement, membership length, and referral source.

Model evaluation balanced interpretability with predictive strength, using metrics like AUC, accuracy, precision, and recall to provide a complete performance picture.

Understanding the Models

Logistic Regression: This model acts as a transparent calculator for risk.

  • Easy to interpret: Clearly shows how factors increase or decrease churn risk.
  • Fast to train: Quick to build, useful for basic understanding or a starting point.
  • Good for diagnostics: Reveals straightforward, linear connections.
  • Limitation: Limited to linear relationships: Struggles with complex or subtle churn patterns.
  • Limitation: Lower accuracy on complex behavior: May miss churners if reasons are not simple.

Random Forest: This model operates as a team of decision-makers, finding intricate patterns.

  • High accuracy: Excels at capturing tricky, non-obvious churn behaviors.
  • Reveals top drivers: Helps understand which factors are most important for churn, even if the how is complex.
  • Resistant to overfitting: Less likely to be misled by random data noise, ensuring reliable real-world predictions.
  • Limitation: Harder to interpret: Explaining exactly how any single prediction was made is challenging.
  • Limitation: Slower to train and deploy: Requires more computing power and time.

Which Model Performs Best?

When the models were put to the test, Logistic Regression emerged as the stronger performer overall, especially in its ability to distinguish between churning and non-churning members. This makes it a more reliable starting point for identifying at-risk individuals.

AUC (Area Under the Curve)

AUC was used to illustrate overall predictive power. This single score (0 to 0.99) indicates how well a model distinguishes between churners and non-churners, with higher scores being better.

  • Logistic Regression achieved an AUC of 0.65, indicating good predictive capability.
  • In comparison, Random Forest had an AUC of 0.61, showing it was slightly less effective.
Confusion Matrix

This scorecard details:

  • True Positives: Members correctly predicted to churn.
  • True Negatives: Members correctly predicted to not churn.
  • False Positives: Members predicted to churn, but didn't (wasted intervention effort).
  • False Negatives: Members predicted to not churn, but did (missed opportunity to intervene).

As the confusion matrices below illustrate, Logistic Regression proved more effective at accurately flagging churners without generating as many misleading alerts. While Random Forest identified a slightly higher percentage of actual churners (recall at 0.353 vs. Logistic Regression's 0.293), Logistic Regression's significantly better precision (0.576 vs. 0.435) and overall accuracy (0.678 vs. 0.614) mean its positive predictions are much more reliable. This makes Logistic Regression the more practical choice for targeted interventions, leading to more efficient and effective retention campaigns.

What Factors Drive Churn Risk? (Feature Importance)

Beyond predicting who will churn, understanding why is critical. The Random Forest model helps by identifying the most influential factors ("feature importance") contributing to churn risk.

As the chart below illustrates, factors related to engagement and membership length dominate, while basic demographic traits have less predictive power.

Here's a breakdown of the top five drivers and what they tell us:

  • Age: Older members tend to be more stable; younger members may have more transient engagement.
  • Engagement Score: Measures attendance consistency. High engagement means less churn, reinforcing habit-building value.
  • Membership Tenure (Months): Churn risk drops sharply after six months. Long-term members are more embedded in routines.
  • Avg Weekly Classes (Last 6 Months): Recent drops in attendance frequency are a key early-warning signal for disengagement.
  • Home Studio Region: Regional differences can reflect unique factors influencing loyalty and experience.

Key Modeling Insights for Action

The modeling phase provided critical insights that move beyond just understanding churn to proactively managing it:

  • High-Accuracy Churn Identification: The Logistic Regression model achieves strong predictive accuracy (0.68), providing a reliable tool to identify members at high risk of churning before they disengage.
  • Targeted Retention Efforts: By minimizing false positives (incorrectly flagged members), the Logistic Regression model ensures retention resources are efficiently focused.
  • Understanding the "Why": Beyond prediction, the model clearly highlights engagement and tenure as the dominant drivers of churn, reinforcing the need for programs that build early habits and foster long-term commitment.
  • Data-Driven Decision Making: Model outputs provide a clear, quantifiable basis for prioritizing retention efforts, allocating resources, and designing personalized strategies, moving away from guesswork.
