Part 3: Churn Prediction Modeling

Turning exploration into prediction

This section focuses on building forecasting tools to spot members at risk of churning before they actually leave. Member behavior and characteristics are used to train machine learning models, estimating individual churn risk and highlighting key influencing factors. The goal is to move from seeing patterns to acting proactively.

View full notebook on GitHub

Modeling Approach: Choosing the Right Tools

To predict member churn, two main types of machine learning models were explored: Logistic Regression and Random Forest. These models learn from data prepared in Part 2: Churn EDA, including engagement, membership length, and referral source.

Model evaluation balances interpretability and predictive strength using AUC, accuracy, precision, and recall.

Understanding the Models

Logistic Regression: The simple, honest baseline

  • What it does: adds up signals and gives you a churn risk score.
  • Why use it: easy to read and explain; quick to train.
  • Limitation: it sees straight lines, so it can miss twisty patterns.

Random Forest: Many small trees voting

  • What it does: captures “if-this-then-that” patterns and how factors work together.
  • Why use it: often more accurate; highlights which features matter most.
  • Limitation: harder to explain a single prediction; a bit heavier to run and deploy.

Which Model Performs Best?

When the models were put to the test, Logistic Regression remains the better overall classifier for targeted retention, with stronger precision and slightly higher accuracy. Random Forest still recovers a few more churners (higher recall) but at the cost of noisier positive flags.

Model AUC Accuracy Precision (churn) Recall (churn)
Logistic Regression 0.650 0.678 0.577 0.294
Random Forest 0.610 0.610 0.426 0.344
Confusion Matrix Takeaways (Test Set)

This scorecard details:

  • True Positives: Members correctly predicted to churn.
  • True Negatives: Members correctly predicted to not churn.
  • False Positives: Members predicted to churn, but didn't (wasted intervention effort).
  • False Negatives: Members predicted to not churn, but did (missed opportunity to intervene).

Logistic Regression proved more effective at accurately flagging churners without generating as many misleading alerts. While Random Forest identified a slightly higher percentage of actual churners (recall at 0.344 vs. Logistic Regression’s 0.294), Logistic Regression’s notably better precision (0.577 vs. 0.426) and overall accuracy (0.678 vs. 0.610) mean its positive predictions are much more reliable. This makes Logistic Regression the more practical choice for targeted interventions, leading to more efficient and effective retention campaigns.

AUC (Area Under the Curve)

AUC was used to illustrate overall predictive power. This score measures overall ranking power from 0.0 to 1.0 (higher is better). It indicates how well a model distinguishes between churners and non-churners, with higher scores being better.

  • Logistic Regression achieved an AUC of 0.65, indicating good predictive capability.
  • In comparison, Random Forest had an AUC of 0.61, showing it was slightly less effective.
Chart Interpretation
  • What’s on the axes:
    The x-axis is False Positive Rate (how often we flag a non-churner), the y-axis is True Positive Rate (how many real churners we catch).
  • The diagonal line:
    That’s random guessing. Any curve above it is doing better than chance. Curves closer to the top-left are stronger (more true positives with fewer false positives).
  • AUC in one sentence:
    AUC is the area under the ROC curve. It’s the chance that the model ranks a random churner higher-risk than a random non-churner. 0.50 = coin flip, 0.60–0.70 = modest but useful, 0.80+ = strong (context matters).
  • Comparing the lines:
    The Logistic Regression curve sits above the Random Forest curve overall, so its AUC is higher (LR ~0.650 vs RF ~0.610 in my tests). That means LR separates churners/non-churners a bit better on average.

What Factors Drive Churn Risk? (Feature Importance)

Beyond prediction, understanding why guides action. For targeting, Logistic Regression is the operational pick due to its higher precision (0.577) and accuracy (0.678) making positive flags more trustworthy. For the why, Random Forest is the clearer lens. It captures non-linearities and interactions, so its feature importances tell a richer, easier-to-explain story than LR’s log-odds coefficients.

The model shows age, recent engagement, and tenure as the strongest signals, with pricing also playing a secondary role.

Here's a breakdown of the top five drivers and what they tell us:

  • Age: Older members tend to be more stable; younger members may have more transient engagement.
  • Avg Weekly Classes (Last 6 Months): Recent drops in attendance frequency are a key early-warning signal for disengagement.
  • Membership Tenure (Months): Churn risk drops sharply after six months. Long-term members are more embedded in routines.
  • Engagement Score: Measures attendance consistency. High engagement means less churn, reinforcing habit-building value.
  • Monthly Membership Cost: Members on higher-priced plans churn slightly more unless engagement stays high—price sensitivity shows up as a secondary driver.

Key Modeling Insights for Action

The modeling phase provided critical insights that move beyond just understanding churn to proactively managing it:

  • Reliable baseline targeting: Logistic Regression reaches 0.678 accuracy with 0.577 precision on churn positives, making its alerts actionable without excessive noise.
  • Targeted Retention Efforts: By minimizing false positives (incorrectly flagged members), the Logistic Regression model ensures retention resources are efficiently focused.
  • Understanding the "Why": Beyond prediction, the model clearly highlights engagement and tenure as the dominant drivers of churn, reinforcing the need for programs that build early habits and foster long-term commitment.
  • Data-Driven Decision Making: Model outputs provide a clear, quantifiable basis for prioritizing retention efforts, allocating resources, and designing personalized strategies, moving away from guesswork.
